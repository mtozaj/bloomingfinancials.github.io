Top Tax Credits California Families Shouldn't Miss

Credits reduce tax dollar for dollar. Here are the big ones California families should review before filing.

Overview

Tax credits can lower what you owe or increase your refund. Some are refundable, which means you can receive money back even if you do not owe tax. Rules change, so use this as a friendly guide and confirm the latest details when you file.

Key California credits

California Earned Income Tax Credit (CalEITC)

For low to moderate income workers with earned income from a job or self-employment. Credit size generally grows with qualifying children. File your state return and include the required worksheet or form for CalEITC.

Young Child Tax Credit (YCTC)

Additional refundable credit for families who qualify for CalEITC and have a child under age 6 at the end of the tax year. Can significantly boost refunds for eligible households.

Foster Youth Tax Credit (FYTC)

Designed to support eligible current or former foster youth who also qualify for CalEITC. If this applies to you, it is worth reviewing because it can be refundable.

California Child and Dependent Care Expenses Credit

Based on a percentage of qualifying care expenses so you can work or look for work. Keep provider details, payment records, and the care recipient's information. Check whether the credit is refundable for the current year when you file.

California Renter's Credit

For eligible renters who paid rent on a principal residence in California for at least half of the year and meet income limits. You cannot claim it if you claimed a homeowner's property tax exemption for the year.

Important federal credits most families review

  • Child Tax Credit (CTC). A per-child credit with phaseouts at higher incomes. The refundable portion depends on current law.
  • Child and Dependent Care Credit. For out-of-pocket daycare, preschool, after-school, or summer day camp used so you can work or look for work.
  • American Opportunity Tax Credit (AOTC). For undergraduate tuition and required fees, with a partially refundable portion.
  • Lifetime Learning Credit (LLC). For a wide range of post-secondary courses, often used for graduate or part-time study.
  • Saver's Credit. For eligible contributions to retirement accounts like IRA or 401(k), subject to income limits.
  • Premium Tax Credit. For health insurance purchased through the exchange (Covered California). If you received advance payments, reconcile with Form 1095-A.

How to get ready

  • Gather wage statements, 1099s, childcare provider info, school billing statements, and health coverage forms.
  • If self-employed, organize income, expenses, and health insurance premiums paid.
  • Keep Social Security numbers or ITINs for everyone on the return.
  • If you moved, confirm California residency status for the year and keep lease or utility records.
Note: Credit amounts and eligibility can change. We will check current-year rules when we prepare your return.
